As we progress, follow along with the copy of the counseling interview rating form provided with this videotape. The counseling interview rating form was designed to assist in teaching and evaluating the microcounseling approach, the counselor's school training described by Allen Ivey and others. The counseling interview rating form has been used for the past six years in beginning and advanced skill level counseling courses, and an accredited training program in school and agency counseling. The counseling interview rating form provides a format for evaluating the five stages of the interview as described by Ivey and the microcounseling skills used in the counseling interview. The counseling interview rating form is divided into six sections that correspond to the five stages plus an additional section on professionalism. Listed within each section are skills or tasks seen in that stage of the interview. The opening section, for example, includes the specific criteria of greeting, role definition, administrative task, and beginning. While watching a videotape to a live session, the person completing the counseling interview rating form tallies the number of times a certain skill is used. Then, values are assigned to indicate the level of mastery achieved for each skill. Reviewers provide a mastery rating of 1, 2, or 3 for each of 43 listed skills that might be used in the session. A score of 1 on a skill that indicates that the skill was observed. A 2 denotes mastery of the skill, and a score of 3 is used when the counselor's observed teaching the skill effectively. If using the counseling interview rating form for assigning grades to a session, please refer to the instructions on the form itself. Grades are calculated by totaling the ratings within a corresponding to 52 or more points. This cut off score was determined by scoring in the mastery range on all essential skills denoted by an X on the counseling interview rating form. Dr. Lori Russell-Chapin will briefly review how to use the counseling interview rating form with her students and then the group will watch a segment of one of the trainees videotaped work with a client. NANCY E. SHERMAN, PH.D.  Although originally designed to be used for evaluating the counseling session, the counseling interview rating form can also provide a detailed format for peer supervision and evaluation. In peer supervision, the trainee presents a videotaped session and peers give feedback using the form. The structured format provides feedback that is consistent and focused which is especially helpful for the inexperienced peer supervisor and observer. In addition, to use as an evaluation tool, the counseling interview rating form is an excellent teaching tool. In beginning skill development classes as students view videotapes of each other's work, they're asked to use the counseling interview rating form to identify the specific skills being used. Trainees learn to identify skills in each stage and the focus can shift from skills in each stage. Students might also be assigned to observe the work of the counselor in the field, and the counseling interview rating form can provide a structure for that observation. To summarize, the counseling interview rating form can be useful in counselor education because it teaches how to classify specific skills and behaviors and evaluate them. It also provides a vocabulary for naming what is occurring in the interview. This form also makes it possible for the student and/or professional to classify his or her behavior in the interview and to examine the intentions of each behavior and the impact the skill had on the client. And finally, the counseling interview rating form provides a format for examining whether the goals of each interview stage were achieved.
