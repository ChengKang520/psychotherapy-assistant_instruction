In our training program leaders receive feedback on their leadership immediately after the group session in a one-hour supervision period. I believe that it is the goal of the supervision to facilitate the beginning leaders learning a process called perceiving, selecting and risking. First, leaders have to perceive what is occurring in order to learn how to understand what's going on, leaders need a broad range of knowledge about groups. It is important to read deeply into the group counseling literature. Observe groups and participate as members. These encourages the development of a foundation of actual and vicarious experience. Yet, even with these knowledge new leaders still need to make the connection between what they know, what is happening in the group and what they can effectively do about it. Therefore, leaders need to be able to mentally generate and select an appropriate response. Here clinical practice, thoughtful reflection and supervisory feedback are the key. Finally, leaders must gain the confidence needed to risk trying out appropriate group interventions. I found over the years that no matter how bright they are, how much reading they've done or how much individual counseling experience they have, beginning leaders are highly anxious. It is the supervisor's job to help reduce and defuse this anxiety.
